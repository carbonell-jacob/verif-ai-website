How to Spot
AI-Generated Images

AI image generators have advanced at a staggering pace. What once produced distorted nightmare fuel now creates portraits indistinguishable — at first glance — from real photographs. But "first glance" is the key phrase. With the right knowledge, the tells are still there.

In 2024 alone, researchers estimated that billions of AI-generated images were published online. From fabricated news photographs to synthetic social media profiles, the ability to spot these images has become a fundamental digital literacy skill — as important as recognising misinformation in a headline.

This guide walks you through the most reliable visual clues, practical verification methods, and the mindset shifts that will help you question what you see before you share it.

1. Examine Hands and Fingers

Hands remain the Achilles' heel of AI image generation. Despite enormous model improvements, synthesising the correct number of fingers, realistic joints, and natural skin folds is computationally difficult. Current models still regularly produce hands with six fingers, fused digits, or fingers that bend in anatomically impossible directions.

When examining a suspicious image, zoom in on any visible hands immediately. Count the fingers. Look at the knuckles — do they form at logical points? Do the nails look uniformly plastic, or do they have the small imperfections of real fingernails?

⚠ Key Checkpoint

Always count fingers in both hands if visible. Even a single extra or missing digit is a strong indicator the image may be AI-generated. Cropped or partially hidden hands can also be a deliberate evasion tactic.

2. Study the Eyes and Teeth

AI-generated faces often feature eyes that are unnaturally symmetrical and uniformly lit, lacking the tiny asymmetries and catchlight variations found in real eyes. Look for pupils that are perfectly round, irises that appear painted on, or a glassy, hyper-detailed sheen that feels more like a rendering than a photograph.

Teeth present a similar challenge for generators. Look for rows of uniformly shaped, perfectly white teeth that lack the subtle spacing, slight discolouration, or minor misalignment found in real smiles. Real teeth catch light unevenly; AI teeth often glow with a plastic uniformity.

👁️

Uncanny Eyes

Perfectly symmetrical pupils, glassy irises, and impossibly even catchlights across both eyes.

😁

Plastic Teeth

Too-white, uniformly spaced teeth with an artificial gloss and no natural variation.

👂

Melted Ears

Ear cartilage structure is complex — AI often softens or distorts it into an amorphous shape.

💇

Impossible Hair

Individual strands merge into a single mass at the hairline or near ears, lacking natural separation.

3. Check the Background and Edges

While AI generators focus computational effort on the primary subject, backgrounds often the seams. Look for text that is garbled or nonsensical — AI models do not understand language, so signs and labels in the background often contain letter-like shapes that spell nothing.

Pay close attention to where the subject meets the background. Real photography produces predictable edge transitions — focus blur, chromatic aberration, motion blur. AI images often have edges that are unnaturally sharp, or have a "cut and paste" quality where the subject seems to float slightly above the scene.

The background is where most generators run out of budget. Blurry signs, walls with non-Euclidean geometry, and reflections that don't match the scene are all telltale signatures of synthesis.

4. Look for Texture and Lighting Inconsistencies

Physical materials — fabric, leather, skin, wood — have characteristic textures built up over time. AI images often render textures that are plausible at a glance but lack the microscopic randomness of the real world. Skin may look airbrushed even when the image is meant to appear unretouched. Fabric folds may follow gravity incorrectly.

Lighting consistency is another critical checkpoint. In real photographs, light comes from a fixed direction and creates predictable shadows across all elements in the scene. In AI images, shadows sometimes originate from different directions, or faces are lit in ways that contradict the ambient lighting of the scene.

5. Use Verification Tools

Your own eye is a powerful first filter, but it shouldn't be your only one. Several tools analyse statistical patterns in pixel data that are invisible to human vision.

  1. 1

    Reverse Image Search

    Use Google Images or TinEye to check whether an image exists elsewhere online with a different context or date.

  2. 2

    AI Detection Classifiers

    Tools like Hive Moderation or Illuminarty analyse image metadata and pixel patterns to assign an AI probability score. Use as supplementary evidence, not definitive proof.

  3. 3

    Inspect EXIF Metadata

    Real photographs typically carry camera model, GPS, lens, and timestamp data. AI-generated images usually have no EXIF data or minimal, inconsistent metadata.

  4. 4

    Check for C2PA Provenance

    Some platforms and cameras embed cryptographically signed information confirming the origin of an image. Look for the CR symbol.

  5. 5

    Cross-Reference the Source

    Anonymous accounts, newly created profiles, and pages with no posting history are all red flags in context.

6. Develop a Skeptical Mindset

Technical skills matter, but the most powerful protection against AI-generated misinformation is epistemic — cultivating a habit of pausing before accepting an image as evidence of anything. Ask yourself: does the emotional charge of this image feel designed to bypass my critical thinking? Is it arriving during a politically sensitive moment?

Uncertainty is not failure. Saying "I'm not sure this is real" and choosing not to share is an act of responsible digital citizenship.

💡 Remember

No single clue is definitive proof that an image is AI-generated. Real photographs can have image artifacts; AI images are getting better at hiding their tells. Always use multiple signals together before drawing a conclusion.
