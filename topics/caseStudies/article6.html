Case Studies of AI in Advertising

Artificial intelligence is transforming the advertising industry in ways that were unimaginable just a decade ago. From personalized ad targeting to AI-generated content, the technology is reshaping how brands reach and interact with consumers. The three peer-reviewed studies below document this shift — and raise important questions about privacy, ethics, and the future of digital advertising.

1. Introduction

AI in advertising is not a single tool — it is a collection of technologies that work together to make advertising smarter, faster, and more precise. These technologies include machine learning, natural language processing, predictive analytics, and computer vision. When applied to advertising, they can analyze massive amounts of consumer data, identify patterns, and deliver highly targeted messages to the right person at the right time.

The three studies reviewed in this article represent key moments in academic research on AI and advertising. Together, they trace the evolution of thinking from early practical frameworks (2018) through reflective academic reviews (2019) to a comprehensive ethical analysis of where the field stands today (2023).

Why This Matters

As AI becomes a central force in how advertising works, understanding both its capabilities and its risks helps consumers recognize when and how they are being targeted — and make more informed decisions about what they see and believe online.

2. Study 1: AI Along the Consumer Journey (Kietzmann, Paschen & Treen, 2018)

Published in the Journal of Advertising Research (Vol. 58, No. 3, pp. 263–267), this study by Jan Kietzmann, Jeannette Paschen, and Emily Treen was one of the first major academic pieces to map out how marketers could use AI specifically along each stage of the consumer decision journey. Written in the aftermath of the Cambridge Analytica scandal, it acknowledged the privacy concerns raised by AI-driven data mining but chose to focus on the constructive applications of the technology.

The authors argued that the consumer journey could be broken down into five stages, and that AI had a distinct role to play at each one:

  1. 1

    Need Recognition

    AI identifies signals that a consumer is beginning to recognize a need — such as search queries, social media activity, or purchase history — and prepares advertisers to respond early.

  2. 2

    Initial Consideration

    AI helps brands appear in the consumer's initial shortlist by analyzing what types of content, platforms, and messages the consumer has engaged with before.

  3. 3

    Active Evaluation

    During this research phase, AI delivers personalized content and comparisons that match the consumer's expressed interests and browsing behavior.

  4. 4

    Purchase

    AI can nudge hesitant buyers with timely offers, retargeted ads, or personalized discounts based on predicted purchase likelihood.

  5. 5

    Post-Purchase

    After a sale, AI monitors consumer sentiment, recommends complementary products, and personalizes follow-up communications to build loyalty.

The IBM Watson Example

One of the most cited examples in this study was the use of IBM Watson by advertisers to generate thousands of individual ad copies automatically. Rather than writing one ad for a broad audience, marketers could train Watson on their brand's tone, target audience profiles, and campaign goals — and let it produce large volumes of unique, personalized ad text at scale. This was an early and concrete demonstration that AI was not just analyzing consumers, but actively creating advertising content for them.

Privacy Warning

The authors noted that as AI collects consumer data from more and more sources — imperceptibly combining online behavior, location data, social media activity, and purchase history — it will eventually become "the ultimate test for privacy." The same technology that makes ads more relevant also enables unprecedented surveillance of consumer behavior.

Key Takeaways from Study 1

  • AI is most powerful in advertising when it is aligned with specific stages of the consumer decision journey, not applied as a general tool.
  • Even in 2018, AI was already being used to generate ad content, not just analyze audiences.
  • The same AI systems that help advertisers also collect large amounts of personal data, raising serious privacy questions.
  • The study called for responsible use of AI that respects consumer privacy rights while still delivering value to both sides.

3. Study 2: AI and Advertising – A Special Section Review (Guo & Vargo, 2019)

Published in the Journal of Advertising (Vol. 48, No. 4), this introduction to a landmark special section on AI in advertising was released to mark the first 25 years of digital advertising. The guest editors compiled a curated collection of peer-reviewed studies across multiple disciplines — each exploring a different dimension of how AI was reshaping the advertising industry.

The special section framed the emergence of AI-powered advertising under the concept of intelligent advertising — a term used to describe advertising systems that can learn, adapt, and make decisions with minimal human input. Unlike traditional digital advertising, which required human planners to set rules and targets, intelligent advertising systems can adjust themselves based on real-time feedback from campaign performance.

This special section will play a role in promoting future research of intelligent advertising and in elevating digital advertising to new heights in the years to come.

Guo & Vargo, Journal of Advertising, 2019

Why This Review Matters

What made this special section significant was not any single finding, but the breadth of research it brought together. By drawing on scholars from computer science, communication, marketing, and psychology, the issue demonstrated that AI in advertising was not a narrow technical problem — it was a multi-disciplinary challenge affecting how society communicates, consumes, and makes decisions.

Key Takeaways from Study 2

  • By 2019, AI in advertising had matured enough to warrant a dedicated special section in a leading academic journal.
  • The concept of "intelligent advertising" highlighted a shift from rule-based systems to self-learning ones.
  • The multi-disciplinary nature of the research signaled that advertising AI required input from many fields — not just technology.
  • The section helped set the research agenda for AI in advertising for years to come.

4. Study 3: AI Advancements, Challenges, and Ethics in Advertising (Gao et al., 2023)

Published in SAGE Open (Vol. 13, No. 4) in November 2023, this open-access study by Biao Gao, Yiming Wang, Huiqin Xie, and Yi Hu conducted the most comprehensive academic review of AI's current role in advertising to date. Using VOSviewer software to map the connections between a large body of published literature, the authors identified four key elements through which AI is transforming advertising:

🎯

Targeting

AI analyzes consumer data to identify who should see an ad, improving precision and reducing wasted spend.

🧩

Personalization

Closely linked to targeting, AI tailors the content of ads to match an individual's preferences, history, and behavior.

✍️

Content Creation

AI generates ad copy, images, videos, and other creative assets — often faster and at greater scale than human teams alone.

📈

Ad Optimization

AI continuously adjusts which ads are shown, to whom, and when — to achieve the highest possible return on investment.

How the Four Elements Work Together

The study found that these four elements do not operate independently. Targeting and Personalization are closely interlinked — together, they determine who sees an ad and what version of the ad they see. Content Creation produces the material that is then delivered through this personalized targeting system. Ad Optimization then monitors the results and feeds data back into the process, continually improving all three previous elements. In this way, AI in advertising functions less like a single tool and more like a self-reinforcing loop.

Ethical Issues Identified

Despite the efficiency gains, Gao et al. emphasized that AI in advertising raises pressing ethical concerns:

  • Privacy: AI systems collect and combine vast amounts of personal data, often without users fully understanding the extent of what is tracked.
  • Algorithmic bias: AI systems trained on biased historical data can replicate and amplify those biases in ad delivery — showing some groups different products, prices, or opportunities than others.
  • Transparency: Consumers are often unaware that the ads they see have been tailored specifically to them based on their behavior, and that AI — not a human — made those decisions.
  • Manipulation risk: Highly personalized advertising can exploit psychological vulnerabilities, making it harder for consumers to distinguish genuine recommendations from AI-driven persuasion.
Key Finding

The authors concluded that while AI has the potential to fundamentally transform the advertising industry for the better, it also introduces challenges and ethical concerns that require active exploration and resolution — to ensure its responsible use.

Key Takeaways from Study 3

  • AI in advertising operates across four interconnected elements: Targeting, Personalization, Content Creation, and Ad Optimization.
  • These elements form a self-reinforcing cycle — each one feeds data back into the others, making the overall system more effective over time.
  • Ethical risks — especially around privacy and bias — are growing alongside the technology's capabilities.
  • Responsible and transparent use of AI in advertising requires deliberate attention from both industry practitioners and policymakers.

5. Key Takeaways Across All Three Studies

Looking at the three studies together, several consistent themes emerge. Whether we are reading a practical 2018 framework, a 2019 academic review, or a 2023 ethical analysis, the same fundamental tensions appear — and they are directly relevant to how ordinary people experience advertising online every day.

  1. 1

    AI makes advertising more powerful — and more invisible

    The more effectively AI targets and personalizes ads, the less obvious it becomes that advertising is happening at all. What feels like a relevant search result or a helpful product suggestion may be a carefully engineered AI-driven nudge.

  2. 2

    AI-generated content is already widespread in advertising

    From the IBM Watson examples in 2018 to the large-scale content generation systems described in 2023, AI has been writing, designing, and optimizing ad content for years. Much of the advertising consumers see today was partly or fully created by an AI system.

  3. 3

    Data collection is the engine behind all of it

    Every AI advertising system depends on collecting large amounts of consumer data. The more data collected, the more precisely ads can be targeted. This creates a direct trade-off between advertising effectiveness and personal privacy.

  4. 4

    Ethical guardrails have not kept pace with the technology

    All three studies acknowledge that the technology is advancing faster than the rules and norms governing its use. Consumers, regulators, and even advertising professionals are still catching up to what AI can do.

  5. 5

    Awareness is the first line of defense

    Understanding that advertising is AI-driven — and knowing how the targeting, personalization, and optimization cycle works — puts consumers in a better position to think critically about what they see online and why they are seeing it.

💡 Remember

When you see an ad that feels surprisingly relevant to something you were thinking about or recently searched for, that is not coincidence. AI advertising systems are designed to feel that way. Recognizing this is the first step to engaging with online content more critically.
