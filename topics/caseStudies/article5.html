Case Studies of AI in Politics

AI is no longer just a tech topic. It is now a major force in politics, and its effects are being felt in elections around the world. From fake audio clips of world leaders to AI tools that help campaigns write ads in seconds, the line between real and fake in politics is getting harder to see.

This article walks through real cases where AI was used in political settings — some that were harmful, some that were surprisingly creative, and all of them worth understanding.

What Is AI in Politics?

When we talk about AI in politics, we mean any use of artificial intelligence to influence, run, or interfere with political activity. This includes things like:

  • Fake audio or video of politicians saying things they never said
  • AI tools that help campaigns write speeches or ads faster
  • Chatbots that answer voters' questions on behalf of a candidate
  • Foreign groups using AI to spread false information about an election
  • AI systems that target specific voters with specific messages

In 2024 alone, more than 80 percent of countries around the world experienced some form of AI-related activity tied to their elections. Most of it involved creating content — fake audio, fake videos, fake endorsements — designed to mislead voters.

Key Point

AI does not create political problems on its own. But it makes it much cheaper and faster for bad actors to spread false information, and much harder for regular people to tell what is real.

Case Study: The Biden Robocall (USA, 2024)

Figure 1: A picture of Joe Biden on the phone.

On January 21, 2024, two days before the New Hampshire Democratic presidential primary, thousands of voters received a phone call. The voice on the line sounded exactly like President Joe Biden.

The caller said: "It's important that you save your vote for the November election. Voting this Tuesday only enables the Republicans." It then left the phone number of a local Democratic Party organizer, seemingly to make it look official.

The problem? Biden never made that call. The voice was a deepfake — an AI-generated clone of his voice, created using voice-cloning software. The goal was to confuse voters into staying home during the primary.

Who Was Behind It?

A political consultant named Steve Kramer, who was working for a rival candidate, admitted to setting up the robocall. He hired a man who used AI software to clone Biden's voice for just $150. The calls reached between 5,000 and 25,000 New Hampshire voters.

The fallout was significant. The Federal Communications Commission proposed a $6 million fine against Kramer. The telecom company that transmitted the calls, Lingo Telecom, agreed to pay a separate $1 million fine. Kramer also faced 26 criminal counts in New Hampshire, including felony voter suppression.

Digital forensics experts who reviewed the clip confirmed it was AI-generated, noting that the voice's pacing sounded unnatural toward the end — a common tell in voice deepfakes. The FCC also moved to ban AI-generated voices in robocalls entirely as a direct response to this incident.

This case was a first: the first known use of an AI deepfake in a US presidential campaign context. Experts called it a "canary in the coal mine" for what elections could face in the future.

Case Study: Imran Khan's AI Speech (Pakistan, 2024)

In Pakistan's February 2024 general election, the political party PTI faced a serious problem. Their leader, former Prime Minister Imran Khan, was in prison and could not speak publicly or campaign. His party had also been banned.

So PTI turned to AI. The party created AI-generated video footage of Imran Khan delivering political speeches — apparently from his prison cell — urging his supporters to vote.

It worked. Despite Khan being behind bars and the party being officially banned, PTI-backed independent candidates won the most seats in Pakistan's National Assembly — a result that had never happened before in the country's history. Independent candidates had never previously secured a majority in the National Assembly.

This case is different from most others in this article because it was not about spreading false information. Khan's supporters knew the speeches were AI-generated. The party used AI as a practical tool to keep their campaign alive under extraordinary circumstances.

Why This Matters

The Pakistan case shows that AI in politics is not always about deception. Sometimes it raises complicated questions: Is it okay to use AI to let a silenced political figure speak? Who gets to decide?

Case Study: Romania's Nullified Election (2024)

In late 2024, Romania held the first round of its presidential election. A far-right candidate named Calin Georgescu came out of nowhere to win the first round, shocking political observers across Europe.

Investigators later found evidence that a Russian-linked operation had used AI and coordinated social media tactics to boost Georgescu's profile online. Huge amounts of content about him were pushed through TikTok and other platforms in what appeared to be an organized foreign influence campaign.

The situation was serious enough that Romania's Constitutional Court took the extraordinary step of nullifying the first-round result and ordering a new vote — one of the only times in modern European history that an election result was thrown out because of suspected foreign digital interference.

This case shows how AI-driven influence campaigns do not have to use deepfakes to cause damage. Coordinated content — real-looking posts, AI-written comments, and targeted promotion — can shift public opinion on their own.

Case Study: AI on the Campaign Trail (USA, 2024)

Not all political AI use is sinister. During the 2024 US presidential race, campaigns on both sides began quietly using AI tools to help with everyday tasks: drafting fundraising emails, testing different versions of ads, and analyzing voter data faster than ever before.

The Republican National Committee released an AI-generated campaign video shortly after President Biden announced his reelection bid. The video depicted a dystopian version of the US under a second Biden term. The RNC voluntarily disclosed that it was AI-generated — something that most experts said would likely not become common practice.

Campaign consultants interviewed in research studies from early 2024 said they were using AI for things like faster A/B testing of messages, generating rough drafts of speeches, and speeding up data analysis. Many described it as a tool that saved time, not one that replaced human judgment.

  1. 1

    Fundraising emails

    AI tools help campaigns write and test email subject lines and donation appeals at scale

  2. 2

    Ad creation

    Campaigns can generate rough video or image ads in minutes instead of days

  3. 3

    Voter targeting

    AI can analyze large datasets to find which messages work best with which groups of voters

  4. 4

    Rapid response

    When a rival says something newsworthy, AI helps campaigns craft a response in minutes

How AI Shapes What Voters Learn

Beyond individual campaigns, there is a broader concern: AI chatbots like ChatGPT, Claude, and Gemini are becoming a go-to source of political information for many people. Researchers tracked this closely during the 2024 US election.

One research team asked more than 12,000 election-related questions to 12 different AI models, nearly every day from July to November 2024. They collected over 16 million responses. What they found was alarming in some ways.

The models' answers to the same questions changed over time — sometimes after a public model update, but sometimes with no clear explanation. The models also subtly shifted their arguments depending on cues about the user's political leaning, even while keeping their answers factually accurate. In other words, the AI might emphasize different facts to a liberal user versus a conservative user.

Only 8 percent of Californians surveyed in 2025 said they were very confident they could tell the difference between real and AI-generated content online. More than half said they were very concerned about deepfakes in elections.

Something to Think About

If millions of people ask AI chatbots about candidates and elections, small biases or shifts in how those tools respond could have a real effect on how people vote — even if no one intended that to happen.

What We Can Do About It

There is no simple fix, but there are things that can help. Governments, tech companies, and regular people all have a role to play.

  • Some countries are passing laws that require campaigns to label AI-generated content
  • The FCC in the US has already banned AI-generated voices in robocalls
  • Tech companies are building tools to detect and flag deepfakes
  • Researchers are calling for regular public audits of how AI chatbots handle political questions
  • Education programs, like this one, help people recognize AI-generated content before they share it

The most important thing any voter can do right now is slow down before sharing political content. Ask where it came from. Ask whether the voice or face in a video seems slightly off. Ask whether the story matches what trusted news sources are saying.

What We've Learned

AI is already changing politics, and it will keep doing so. The cases in this article — from a $150 voice clone used to suppress votes in New Hampshire, to an imprisoned leader speaking through AI-generated video, to a whole election being thrown out over suspected AI interference — show just how wide the impact can be.

AI is not inherently political, and it is not inherently bad. But in the wrong hands, or even just in careless ones, it can cause real damage to the way democracies function. The best defense is an informed public that knows what to look for.

💡 Remember

If a video, audio clip, or image of a politician feels surprising or hard to believe — stop and check before sharing it. AI makes fakes cheaper and easier than ever. A few seconds of doubt could save a lot of people from being misled.
