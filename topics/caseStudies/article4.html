AI-generated misinformation is no longer a future concern. It has already happened — multiple times — causing real confusion, financial damage, and threats to democracy. The incidents below are documented, verified cases from 2022 to 2025.

1. The Pope's Puffer Jacket (March 2023)

Figure 1: An AI-generated image of Pope Francis wearing a puffer jacket.

In March 2023, an image of Pope Francis wearing a stylish white puffer jacket went viral on social media, racking up millions of views. Many users, including celebrities, believed the photo was real. It was entirely fake — generated using the AI image tool Midjourney by a 31-year-old construction worker from Chicago who said he just thought it would be funny.

The image was first posted to the r/midjourney subreddit on March 24, 2023, under the title "The Pope Drip." From there it spread to Twitter and TikTok. Celebrity Chrissy Teigen tweeted that she believed it without question, saying "no way am I surviving the future of technology."

Why It Spread

The image was plausible — the Pope is known for being more open than past leaders, which made the unusual jacket feel believable to some. The AI rendering was also realistic enough that small errors (distorted hands, blurry coffee cup, glasses merging into shadow) were easy to miss on a fast social media scroll.

Key Takeaways

  • The image was described by reporters as one of the first mass-level AI misinformation events.
  • Nearly half of 240,000 users in one online poll initially believed it was real.
  • The creator said it was "definitely scary" that people accepted it without question.
  • Close inspection revealed warped hands, a floating crucifix, and unnatural skin texture — all common signs of AI generation.

History may regard the Balenciaga Pope as the first truly viral misinformation event fueled by AI image technology — and a sign of worse to come.

TIME Magazine, March 2023

2. The Fake Pentagon Explosion (May 2023)

Figure 2: An AI-generated image of an explosion at the Pentagon.

On May 22, 2023, a verified Twitter account called "Bloomberg Feed" — impersonating the real Bloomberg news agency — posted an image claiming to show a large explosion near the Pentagon in Washington D.C. The image was almost certainly AI-generated. Within minutes, it had spread across social media and was picked up by real news outlets, including an Indian television network that aired it live.

The Arlington County fire department quickly confirmed that no explosion had taken place. But in the brief window before the truth spread, the US stock market reacted. The S&P 500 dropped roughly 0.3% and the Dow Jones fell about 80 points before recovering just minutes later once the hoax was debunked.

Market Impact

Bloomberg described it as "possibly the first instance of an AI-generated image moving the market." The financial disruption lasted only about ten minutes, but it demonstrated how quickly fake content can cause real economic harm.

How Investigators Spotted It

  • The building in the image did not closely resemble the actual Pentagon.
  • The fence in the image merged unnaturally into the crowd barriers.
  • There were no firsthand witnesses, videos, or corroborating posts from people near the area.
  • Open-source investigators at Bellingcat flagged it almost immediately on Twitter.

What Made It Worse

At the time, Twitter had recently changed its verification system to allow anyone to pay for a blue check mark. This meant the fake "Bloomberg Feed" account carried a trust badge despite not being affiliated with Bloomberg at all. Russian state media outlet RT also shared the image to its 3 million Twitter followers before deleting the post.

3. The Zelenskyy Surrender Deepfake (March 2022)

Figure 3: Screenshot of Zelensky's surrender deepfake video.

On March 16, 2022, during the early weeks of Russia's invasion of Ukraine, a deepfake video appeared online showing Ukrainian President Volodymyr Zelenskyy standing at a podium and telling his soldiers to lay down their arms and surrender to Russia. The video was placed on the hacked website of Ukrainian broadcaster Ukraine 24, and the same message was pushed through the channel's live news ticker.

Zelenskyy responded quickly, posting a real video on his Telegram channel to confirm that the surrender message was fake and that Ukraine would not give up. Facebook, YouTube, and Twitter all removed the deepfake for violating their policies. On Russian social media platform VKontakte, however, it continued to circulate.

Context That Made It Dangerous

The Ukrainian government had actually warned citizens two weeks earlier that a fake surrender video might appear. Even so, experts noted the real risk: Ukrainian internet connections were unreliable due to the conflict, and people in war zones do not always have the time or resources to stop and fact-check a video before acting on it.

Key Takeaways

  • This was the first major use of a deepfake in an active armed conflict.
  • The video was not particularly convincing — Zelenskyy's accent was off, and the lip sync was imperfect — but it still caused confusion.
  • Researchers warned that as deepfake quality improves, debunking will become much harder and slower.
  • The incident showed how AI misinformation and hacking can be used together as a warfare tool.

4. The Fake Biden Robocall (January 2024)

On January 21, 2024 — two days before the New Hampshire presidential primary election — thousands of New Hampshire residents received a robocall. The voice on the call sounded like President Joe Biden. It told recipients not to vote on Tuesday and to "save your vote for the November election."

The call was an AI-generated voice clone. Experts confirmed it was almost certainly a deepfake. The New Hampshire Attorney General's office launched an investigation and called it "an unlawful attempt to disrupt the New Hampshire Presidential Primary Election." The calls also illegally spoofed their caller ID to appear to come from a former New Hampshire Democratic Party chair.

Who Was Behind It

Investigators traced the calls to a political consultant named Steve Kramer and a company called Life Corporation, which used a telecom firm called Lingo Telecom to originate the calls. Kramer later admitted to commissioning the robocall, claiming he did it to raise awareness about AI deepfakes — not to suppress votes. He was later indicted on criminal charges in four New Hampshire counties, including 13 felony counts of voter suppression. The FCC issued a cease-and-desist to Lingo Telecom and proposed a $6 million fine.

Key Takeaways

  • It was one of the first documented cases of AI being used to interfere with a US election.
  • Between 5,000 and 25,000 calls were estimated to have gone out.
  • The FCC responded by speeding up plans to formally ban AI-generated robocalls.
  • The League of Women Voters filed a federal lawsuit on behalf of voters who received the calls.
  • Experts warned this was "surely a sign of things to come" in future elections globally.

5. AI Disaster Footage During Hurricanes Milton and Helene (October 2024)

During Hurricanes Milton and Helene in October 2024, AI-generated videos circulated widely across social media, presented as real footage of the storms. These videos showed dramatic scenes of flooding, destruction, and rescue operations that never actually happened or were taken from entirely different events.

The false content created confusion about where relief was needed, what areas were affected, and how bad the damage was. Emergency communication researchers noted that AI misinformation during disasters is particularly dangerous because people are already afraid, communications infrastructure may be damaged, and the need for fast decisions is high.

🌀

Wrong location

Some videos showed flooding in areas that were not affected by the storms at all.

🎬

Too dramatic

AI-generated disaster scenes often look like movie effects — unnaturally vivid and perfectly framed.

🔍

No source

Real disaster footage usually comes from news cameras, rescue teams, or locals. Fake clips appear with no clear origin.

📵

No corroboration

If dozens of journalists and residents are in an area, but only one video shows something extreme, that is a red flag.

Key Takeaways

  • False videos misled people about which areas needed help most.
  • Researchers flagged AI disaster misinformation as a growing threat to emergency communications and disaster response.
  • A similar pattern occurred during a tsunami alert in June 2025, when AI-generated videos of giant waves crashing into coastlines received millions of views before moderators could respond.

6. The Rise of AI-Powered Fake News Websites

The problem of AI misinformation is not limited to individual viral posts. Since 2023, there has been a large and documented growth in entire fake news websites that are built and run using AI with little or no human oversight.

According to NewsGuard, which tracks fake news sites, the number of AI-enabled fake news sites increased tenfold in 2023 alone. These sites generate large volumes of articles automatically, often copying the style and layout of real news outlets. They target topics where emotions run high — health, politics, disasters, and elections.

Real Example: Newspaper AI Blunder (May 2025)

In May 2025, the Chicago Sun-Times and Philadelphia Inquirer published a syndicated special section that included a summer reading list recommending books that did not exist. The section was prepared by a journalist using AI to assist with the content, who admitted he failed to fact-check the AI's output. The fictional books were presented as real recommendations to readers.

Key Takeaways

  • AI can generate false content at a speed and volume that human fact-checkers cannot easily match.
  • Fake news sites are harder to spot when they are professionally designed and regularly updated.
  • Even real journalists working at reputable outlets have published AI-generated content without verifying it.
  • Checking the publication, its history, its ownership, and whether other credible outlets report the same story remains essential.

Common Patterns Across All Incidents

Looking at all the cases above, certain patterns appear again and again. Recognizing these patterns can help you catch AI misinformation before it affects you.

  1. 1

    Emotional urgency

    Every incident involved content designed to trigger a fast emotional response — fear, shock, or disbelief. That urgency is what makes people share before checking.

  2. 2

    Impersonation of trusted sources

    Fake Bloomberg accounts, hacked news websites, cloned presidential voices — in each case, the misinformation borrowed authority from a real and trusted source.

  3. 3

    Fast spread before debunking

    In most cases, the false content spread widely within minutes. Corrections came later — after many people had already seen and shared the original.

  4. 4

    Exploiting high-attention moments

    Elections, wars, natural disasters, and celebrity news — AI misinformation consistently targets moments when people are already paying close attention and emotions are high.

  5. 5

    Real-world consequences

    These were not just online events. They moved stock markets, triggered criminal charges, disrupted disaster response, and threatened election integrity.

What We've Learned

The incidents in this article are not edge cases. They represent a clear and growing trend. AI tools have made it easier, cheaper, and faster to create fake images, videos, audio, and text that can fool large numbers of people — at least briefly. In every case, the misinformation caused some level of real harm before it was corrected.

The good news is that most of these incidents were eventually debunked, often within minutes or hours, thanks to open-source investigators, platform moderators, and ordinary users who spotted the signs and raised the alarm. The key skills that helped — looking closely at image details, checking multiple sources, asking who made the content and why — are skills anyone can develop.

💡 What You Can Do

Before sharing any image, video, or audio clip that seems surprising: check the source account, reverse-image-search photos, look for the same story on multiple credible outlets, and look closely for AI artifacts like distorted hands, unnatural lighting, or voices that sound slightly off. Slowing down for 60 seconds before sharing can stop false content from spreading further.
