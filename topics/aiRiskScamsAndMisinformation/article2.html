Misinformation vs Disinformation

Vince Bobiles Content Manager
April 2026
7 min read
In This Article

  • 3. How AI Amplifies Both
  • 4. Real-World Examples

The words "misinformation" and "disinformation" are often used interchangeably, but they mean very different things. Understanding the distinction between them is one of the most important steps in becoming a more critical consumer of digital media — especially in an era where artificial intelligence makes it easier than ever to produce and spread false content.

Both misinformation and disinformation involve the spread of false or misleading information, but they differ in one critical way: intent. This article breaks down what each term means, how AI is making both more dangerous, and why knowing the difference changes how you should respond.

Defining the Two Terms

Misinformation

Misinformation is false or inaccurate information that is shared without the intent to deceive. The person spreading it genuinely believes the content is true. They are not trying to manipulate anyone — they are simply mistaken.

A common example is when someone shares a health tip on social media that sounds reasonable but is actually based on outdated or inaccurate science. The person sharing it wants to help others, but the information itself is wrong. Another example is forwarding a news story that turns out to be from a satirical website, not realizing it was meant as humor rather than fact.

Disinformation

Disinformation is false information that is deliberately created and spread with the intent to deceive, mislead, or cause harm. Unlike misinformation, the person or group behind disinformation knows the content is false and distributes it on purpose.

Disinformation campaigns are often coordinated and strategic. They may be carried out by political organizations, state-sponsored actors, scam networks, or individuals with a specific agenda. The goal could be to influence an election, damage a competitor's reputation, create social division, or profit from deception.

Key Point

The same piece of false content can be both disinformation and misinformation at different stages. It starts as disinformation when someone creates it intentionally, then becomes misinformation when unsuspecting people share it believing it to be true.

Malinformation

There is also a third related term worth knowing: malinformation. This refers to information that is based on reality but is used out of context, exaggerated, or shared at a specific time to cause harm. For example, leaking someone's private information to damage their reputation is malinformation — the facts may be real, but the intent is to harm.

Key Differences at a Glance

The following comparison highlights the core distinctions between misinformation and disinformation:

  1. 1

    Intent

    Misinformation is shared without the intent to deceive — the person believes it is true. Disinformation is created and spread deliberately to mislead.

  2. 2

    Source

    Misinformation often originates from ordinary people who encountered false content and passed it along. Disinformation typically comes from organized actors — political groups, state agencies, or scam operations — with a clear agenda.

  3. 3

    Awareness

    People spreading misinformation do not know the content is false. People spreading disinformation are fully aware that they are distributing fabricated or manipulated material.

  4. 4

    Goal

    Misinformation has no strategic goal — it spreads through carelessness, confusion, or good intentions gone wrong. Disinformation has a specific objective: to influence opinions, sow confusion, gain power, or generate profit.

  5. 5

    Harm

    Both can cause significant harm, but disinformation is generally more dangerous because it is designed to exploit vulnerabilities and maximize its impact on the target audience.

How AI Amplifies Both

Artificial intelligence has dramatically increased the volume, speed, and sophistication of both misinformation and disinformation. Here is how AI contributes to each:

AI and Misinformation

AI tools like chatbots and text generators can produce plausible but inaccurate content. When users ask AI models for information, the models sometimes generate responses that sound confident and authoritative but contain factual errors — a phenomenon known as "hallucination." Users who trust this output and share it without verification become unwitting spreaders of misinformation.

AI-generated images can also create misinformation unintentionally. When someone generates a realistic image for entertainment and shares it online without labeling it as AI-made, other people may mistake it for a real photograph and spread it as evidence of events that never occurred.

AI and Disinformation

For those with malicious intent, AI is a force multiplier. Disinformation campaigns that once required teams of writers, graphic designers, and coordinators can now be executed by a handful of people using AI tools. Specific ways AI powers disinformation include:

  • Deepfake videos: AI can generate realistic video footage of public figures saying things they never said. These videos can be used to fabricate political statements, create fake endorsements, or manufacture scandals.
  • Voice cloning: With just a few seconds of audio, AI can clone someone's voice convincingly enough to make phone calls or voice messages that sound authentic. This is used in scams targeting families, businesses, and public institutions.
  • Bot networks: AI-powered social media bots can create thousands of fake accounts that post, like, share, and comment on disinformation content, making it appear more popular and credible than it actually is.
  • Automated content farms: AI text generators can produce hundreds of fake news articles per day, each tailored to different audiences, platforms, and languages, making disinformation campaigns truly global in reach.
Key Point

AI does not just make disinformation easier to create — it makes it easier to personalize. Campaigns can now target specific communities, demographics, and even individuals with content tailored to exploit their beliefs and emotional triggers.

Real-World Examples

Misinformation Example: AI "Hallucinations" Spread as Fact

In multiple documented cases, users have asked AI chatbots for information about historical events, medical conditions, or legal matters and received confidently stated but completely false answers. When users then shared these AI-generated "facts" on social media or used them in reports, the false information spread to wider audiences who had no reason to question it.

Misinformation Example: Fake Disaster Photos

After natural disasters, AI-generated images depicting exaggerated or entirely fabricated destruction have gone viral on social media. People shared these images out of genuine concern, not realizing they were AI-generated. The false images diverted attention from actual affected areas and undermined trust in legitimate disaster reporting.

Disinformation Example: Election Deepfakes

During election periods, deepfake audio recordings of political candidates have been created and distributed through messaging apps and social media. In one notable case in 2024, an AI-generated robocall impersonating a U.S. presidential candidate was sent to thousands of voters in New Hampshire, urging them not to vote in the primary. The call was traced back to a deliberate disinformation operation.

Disinformation Example: Corporate Fraud

Organized disinformation campaigns have used AI-generated fake news articles and social media posts to manipulate stock prices. By creating convincing but false reports about companies — such as fake merger announcements or fabricated lawsuits — bad actors have profited from the resulting market reactions before the truth could be established.

Why the Distinction Matters

Understanding whether false content is misinformation or disinformation is not just an academic exercise — it changes how you should respond.

  • Responding to misinformation: When someone shares false content without knowing it is false, the appropriate response is education, not punishment. A polite correction with a link to a reliable source is usually more effective than accusation or shaming. People who spread misinformation are often willing to correct themselves once they learn the truth.
  • Responding to disinformation: When false content is being spread deliberately, the response needs to be more assertive. This may involve reporting the content to the platform, flagging it with fact-checking organizations, and warning others in your network. Understanding that the content was designed to manipulate helps you resist its emotional pull.
  • Policy implications: Governments and platforms are developing different strategies for each. Misinformation is often addressed through media literacy programs and algorithmic changes, while disinformation may require legal action, account suspensions, and coordinated responses from intelligence agencies.
  • Personal accountability: Recognizing the difference helps you evaluate your own sharing habits. Before reposting content, ask yourself: do I know this is true? Could someone be trying to manipulate me? Am I about to become an unwitting participant in a disinformation campaign?

How to Identify and Respond

Whether you encounter misinformation or disinformation, these practical steps will help you evaluate and respond appropriately:

  1. 1

    Check the source

    Where did the content come from? Is the original poster a verified journalist, a known organization, or an anonymous account? Content from unverified or newly created accounts deserves extra scrutiny.

  2. 2

    Look for corroboration

    Is the same story being reported by multiple reputable news outlets? If a dramatic claim appears only on one website or social media post, it is more likely to be false.

  3. 3

    Assess the emotional tone

    Both misinformation and disinformation often use highly emotional language to bypass critical thinking. If content makes you feel outraged, terrified, or euphoric, pause and evaluate it carefully before reacting.

  4. 4

    Consider the intent

    Ask yourself: who benefits from this content being spread? If it clearly serves a political agenda, promotes a product, or targets a specific group, it may be disinformation. If it appears to be a genuine mistake, it is more likely misinformation.

  5. 5

    Use fact-checking tools

    Organizations like Snopes, PolitiFact, and AFP Fact Check maintain searchable databases of debunked claims. Before sharing questionable content, take a moment to search for it on these platforms.

  6. 6

    Report and correct

    If you identify false content, report it to the platform. If someone you know has shared misinformation, let them know respectfully. If you discover you shared something false yourself, correct it publicly — accountability builds trust.

What We've Learned

Misinformation and disinformation are two distinct but related threats to our information environment. Misinformation is false content shared without malicious intent — the person passing it along believes it is true. Disinformation is false content created and spread deliberately to deceive, manipulate, or cause harm.

AI has amplified both. Through hallucinating chatbots and unlabeled synthetic images, AI contributes to the accidental spread of misinformation. Through deepfakes, voice cloning, bot networks, and automated content farms, AI supercharges deliberate disinformation campaigns.

Knowing the difference matters because it changes how you should respond. Misinformation calls for gentle correction and education. Disinformation calls for reporting, flagging, and active resistance. In both cases, the most powerful tool you have is the habit of pausing to think critically before believing or sharing anything you encounter online.

Remember

Not all false information is created equal. Understanding whether content is misinformation or disinformation helps you respond wisely — correcting honest mistakes with kindness, and confronting deliberate deception with vigilance.
