AI Regulation Trends

Chris Ivan Maghirang UI Designer

7 min read
In This Article

  • 2. Mandatory Labeling of AI-Generated Content

Increasing Transparency and “Explainable AI” Requirements

A major trend in AI regulation is requiring companies to make their AI systems more transparent. This means organizations must explain how their AI makes decisions, what data it uses, and why certain outputs are produced.

Figure 1: Regulations

People want to understand how AI systems work, especially when they affect jobs, loans, health, or legal decisions. Instead of black-box AI, regulators are pushing for models that can be explained and audited.

Example

  • Companies must provide documentation showing how their AI makes hiring or recommendation decisions.
  • AI chatbots and tools need to disclose when a user is interacting with AI.
  • Some countries require businesses to publish “AI impact assessments” before deploying AI in sensitive areas.

Mandatory Labeling of AI-Generated Content

Another growing trend is requiring labels on AI-generated text, images, videos, and audio. This is to help the public identify whether content was created by a human or an AI tool.

Figure 2:AI-Generated

AI tools can now create extremely realistic content, so labeling helps prevent confusion and maintains trust in media.

Examples

  • Social media platforms adding “AI-generated” tags on images created using generative tools.
  • Video-editing AI automatically embedding metadata showing the content is AI-made.
  • Regulations requiring political ads to reveal when AI was used in the messaging or visuals.

Establishing AI Licensing and Certification Systems

Governments are starting to treat advanced AI systems like other regulated technologies by requiring companies to obtain licenses, certifications, or approvals before releasing certain AI models.

This ensures that powerful AI systems meet safety, accuracy, and ethical standards before being used by the public.

Examples

  • High-risk AI systems (like biometric scanning tools) requiring government approval before deployment.
  • Companies undergoing third-party audits to certify their AI models for fairness and reliability.
  • Specialized licenses for developers building large, general-purpose AI systems.
